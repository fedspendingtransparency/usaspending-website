There are many examples of a loan in everyday life. Some loans are simple and very temporary, such as borrowing a pen from the store cashier to sign a receipt. Some loans are longer-term and more complex, like Federal Student Aid or small business loans.

In the world of government spending, a loan is defined as “a federal award from the government that the borrower will eventually have to pay back.”

On USAspending.gov’s Advanced Search, loans are found under Award Type; users can search for two different types of loans: direct loans and guaranteed/insured loans.

Types of Loans

Direct Loans

Direct loans are those made for a specific time period with a reasonable expectation of repayment; they may or may not require interest payments.

Guaranteed/Insured Loans

Guaranteed or insured loans require the federal government to pay the bank and take over the loan if the borrower defaults.

The Value of a Loan

There are two data elements used to measure the value of a loan: the face value of the loan and the loan subsidy cost.

Face Value of a Loan

The face value of a loan is the total amount of the loan.

Because federal loans are expected to be repaid, the face value of a loan is not considered federal spending (and for loan guarantees, the face value is not even directly provided by the government, but instead from a third-party financial institution).

The face value of a loan is not included in obligations or outlays.

Loan Subsidy Cost

The loan subsidy cost is the government’s estimate of the loan’s likely cost to the government, in net present value terms.
Loan subsidy costs can be positive, negative, or zero depending on whether the government expects to lose money, gain money, or break even on a loan.

Loan subsidy cost is calculated based on a credit model specific to the program and, in some cases, the recipient’s characteristics or credit history. Loan subsidy cost allows the government to budget for potential defaults on loans.

Additionally, positive loan subsidy costs are included in obligations and outlays.
