For this series, we asked our USAspending Service Desk to answer the most commonly-asked questions about USAspending.gov’s open data.

Today’s topic: Parent/Child Recipients—an essential aspect of understanding relationships in award data.

But don’t worry, we won’t need to consult a therapist for this topic. We’re talking about the relationship between two levels of recipient data from award data, as seen in our [advanced search](/search): parent recipient and child recipient.

Think about the way tree branches grow from one central trunk: larger branches are directly connected to the trunk, and smaller branches grow from those larger branches, and some of those smaller branches have even smaller branches, and so on and so forth.

Using our tree example, the parent recipients are the larger branches, coming directly from the trunk. The child recipients are the smaller branches, directly connected to the parent recipients.

If you’re not a nature lover, think about it this way: recipient “parents” are the highest point in the hierarchy. For example, state governments and state-level agencies: “State of Texas” is the parent recipient and “Texas Department of Motor Vehicles” is the child recipient."

<section id="parent-recipient">
## Parent Recipient

A parent recipient is identified when an award lists one or more recipients as their parent organization; parent organizations are also sometimes identified in SAM.gov.
</section>

<section id="child-recipient">
## Child Recipient

A child recipient is a recipient that has a parent organization listed in awards.
</section>

<section id="recipient">
## Recipient

This recipient does not have a parent organization listed in awards. 
</section>

<section id="a-complex-family-tree">
## A Complex Family Tree

A single entity (such as state/local government or corporation) can be a parent, child, and recipient, depending on how various awards are reported.

It can be difficult to determine the total obligations for a complex entity; in the case of the recipient, it’s based on the organizational structure.

Sometimes recipients can be associated with multiple parents in a dataset if:

A legal entity was bought or sold to another legal entity. 

An internal restructuring of a large company caused a legal entity to list another parent. 

A new entity was created to be used solely as the parent legal entity for a large organization. 

Data entry errors of parent information in [SAM.gov](https://SAM.gov) (or DUN and Bradstreet) that were not immediately corrected.

In addition, the names of companies are managed by transactions, subawards, and SAM data where each record lists a <GlossaryLink term="uei" label="UEI" /> and an ultimate parent UEI.
</section>

<section id="adding-it-all-up">
## Adding It All Up

The USAspending Service Desk recommends you use the [Recipient Profile pages](/recipients) to assess the UEIs and award counts to get an idea of how the awards are related and find the best total. If you’re using the site, you may also want to use the "View awards to this recipient" link (under the “Total Awarded Amount” in the recipient profile) to locate the awards in Advanced Search and download the data. If you’re using the downloaded data, you’ll want to total the federal_action_obligation amount, both positive and negative.
</section>

<section id="lost-in-the-woods">
## Lost in the Woods? There’s Help!

The parent/child relationships between recipients can be complex. If you have questions about entity information, the USAspending Service Desk recommends you reach out to SAM.gov.

To contact SAM.gov, visit [FSD.gov](https://FSD.gov) to search frequently asked questions, chat live with a support agent, submit a web form, or call for assistance.
</section>
